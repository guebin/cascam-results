Computational Cost Analysis

We analyze the computational cost of various Class Activation Mapping (CAM) methods, including the proposed CasCAM approach. The total cost of generating CAMs for \(N\) images can be decomposed into two components:

\[T_{\text{total}} = T_{\text{train}} + N \times t_{\text{inference}}\]

where \(T_{\text{train}}\) is the one-time training cost (amortized over all images) and \(t_{\text{inference}}\) is the per-image inference time. Understanding this trade-off is crucial for selecting the appropriate method based on dataset size and computational constraints.

Hardware Environment: NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80GB GPU, AMD EPYC 7742 64-Core Processor (128 threads), 503 GB RAM. Input images resized to \(512 \times 512\) pixels.

1. Inference Time per Image (\(t_{\text{inference}}\))

Inference time refers to the time required to generate a CAM for a single image after training is complete. Different CAM methods have vastly different inference characteristics:

  • Gradient-based methods (CAM, GradCAM, etc.): Require only a single forward and backward pass through the network. This is extremely fast (~0.025s).
  • Perturbation-based methods (ScoreCAM, AblationCAM): Require multiple forward passes, systematically masking different regions of the image.
  • CasCAM: As an iterative method, requires reading pre-computed CAMs from each iteration and combining them. The inference time scales linearly with the number of iterations.

For CasCAM, the inference time can be approximated as:

\[t_{\text{CasCAM}} \approx 0.015 + 0.024 \times n_{\text{iter}} \quad \text{(seconds)}\]

2. Training Time (\(T_{\text{train}}\))

Training time is a one-time cost required before inference can begin. All CAM methods require at least one round of classifier training (iteration 1), which takes approximately 20.7 minutes in our setup.

Key insight: CasCAM's cascade learning requires training multiple classifiers sequentially (one per iteration). However, training time does not increase linearly with iterations because:

  • CasCAM progressively erases discriminative features from images
  • This makes classification increasingly difficult in later iterations
  • Early stopping triggers more frequently, reducing training epochs

5. Break-even Analysis

The break-even point \(N^*\) is the dataset size at which CasCAM becomes computationally equivalent to another method. For \(N > N^*\), CasCAM is more efficient.

\[N^* = \frac{T_{\text{CasCAM}}^{\text{train}} - T_{\text{other}}^{\text{train}}}{t_{\text{other}}^{\text{inf}} - t_{\text{CasCAM}}^{\text{inf}}}\]

Example calculation for CasCAM vs FullGrad:

\[N^* = \frac{5664\text{s} - 1244\text{s}}{4.562\text{s} - 0.253\text{s}} = \frac{4420\text{s}}{4.309\text{s}} \approx 1,026 \text{ images}\]
Comparison Break-even Point \(N^*\) Interpretation
CasCAM vs CAM/GradCAM Never CasCAM always slower (gradient methods have both lower training and inference cost)
CasCAM vs ScoreCAM \(N > 6,563\) CasCAM faster for very large datasets
CasCAM vs AblationCAM \(N > 5,589\) CasCAM faster for very large datasets
CasCAM vs FullGrad \(N > 1,026\) CasCAM faster for medium-to-large datasets

6. Practical Recommendations

Based on the above analysis, we provide the following guidelines for method selection:

Scenario Recommended Method Rationale
Real-time applications CAM, GradCAM ~0.025s per image; minimal latency
Small-scale analysis (\(N < 1,000\)) FullGrad Good localization without additional training overhead
Large-scale analysis (\(N > 1,000\)) CasCAM Training cost amortized; superior localization accuracy
Accuracy-speed trade-off CasCAM (iter=3-5) Reduced training time while still improving over single-pass methods
Summary: CasCAM's computational profile makes it particularly well-suited for large-scale analysis tasks where the one-time training cost can be amortized over many images. For datasets with more than ~1,000 images, CasCAM offers better localization accuracy than FullGrad at comparable or lower total computational cost.
